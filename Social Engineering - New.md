# Introduction

A social engineering attack is a cyberattack that manipulates people into revealing sensitive information or granting system access. It exploits human psychology—such as trust, helpfulness, and fear—to bypass technical defenses. This technique is widespread and leads to significant financial and data losses.

**Social engineering** is the manipulation of individuals into revealing confidential or sensitive information, often by exploiting human psychology rather than technical hacking methods.

## Impact:

- Theft of user credentials and unauthorized network access.

- Malware infections.

- Data breaches and exposure of confidential information.

## Why Social Engineering is effective?

- Exploits natural human trust and the sense of urgency.

- Leverages fear, greed, or the desire to help others.

- Targets users who may be unaware of evolving attack methods.

<!-- -->

- **Humans Are Easier Targets:** It’s often simpler for attackers to manipulate people than to breach complex technical security controls.

- **High Success Rate:** Many security breaches, even in large corporations, are initiated via social engineering attacks.

- **Common Goals of Social Engineering Attacks (e.g., phishing, vishing):**

  - Trick victims into clicking malicious links

  - Extract sensitive information

  - Install malware on systems\
    These can lead to full network compromise.

## Common Warning Signs

- Urgency: “Act now or lose access!”

- Offers: “You’ve won a prize!”

- Authority: “This is IT support/management…”

- Generic greetings: “Dear user…”

- Spelling and grammar mistakes

- Unexpected attachments or login links

- Requests for confidential data over email/phone

## Common Targets:

- Help desk staff

- Technical support personnel

- System administrators

- Non-technical employees unaware of security risks

Social engineers **exploit human trust, ignorance, fear, or willingness to help** to achieve their goals.

## Vulnerable Behaviours

- **Inherent trust in people**: People often assume others are honest by default.

- **Lack of awareness**: Employees unaware of social engineering techniques are easy targets.

- **Fear tactics**: Victims may comply due to fear of penalties or threats.

- **Greed or incentives**: Attackers may offer fake rewards or opportunities.

- **Moral obligation**: People may help simply because they were asked.

## Organizational Weaknesses That Increase Risk

- Inadequate security awareness training

- Poor access control and information governance

- Disconnected or siloed departments

- Absence of or weak enforcement of security policies

## Process

1.  **Reconnaissance (Research)**\
    Gather information on the target organization (e.g., via social media, websites, dumpster diving).

2.  **Target Selection**\
    Identify specific employees who may be vulnerable (e.g., new hires, disgruntled staff).

3.  **Relationship Building**\
    Establish trust via calls, emails, or in-person interactions (e.g., pretending to be IT support).

4.  **Exploitation**\
    Leverage the trust to extract sensitive data, credentials, or access to systems.

## Human-based/Psychological

**🎭 Category: Human-Based Social Engineering Attacks**

**Definition:**\
Human-based social engineering attacks involve **direct interaction, manipulation, impersonation**, and **physical tactics** to deceive individuals into revealing sensitive information or granting unauthorized access.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**🧍‍♂️ 1. Impersonation-Based Attacks**

Attackers pretend to be someone trustworthy (employee, executive, tech support, vendor) to exploit human trust and gain access.

**Common Impersonation Scenarios:**

- **Internal Employee:** Dressed in business attire or uniforms to access restricted areas.

- **Executive/VIP:** Claims urgency or importance to override security checks.

- **IT Support/Help Desk:** Requests passwords or remote access for “troubleshooting.”

- **Law Enforcement/Authority Figures:** Uses fear or compliance to demand access.

- **Third-Party Authorization:** Claims another employee approved the access.

**Social Media Impersonation Tactics:**

- Create fake profiles on LinkedIn, Facebook, or Twitter.

- Join employee groups and extract data to enhance phishing or identity theft.

- Launch spear phishing attacks using detailed, scraped information.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**🧠 2. Psychological Manipulation Attacks**

These techniques exploit **human psychology** — urgency, fear, authority, helpfulness, or curiosity.

**Tactics Include:**

- **Phishing & Spear Phishing:** Targeted or mass messages prompting users to click links or reveal data.

- **Vishing (Voice Phishing):** Impersonating a bank, IT staff, or manager on a phone call.

- **Pretexting:** Inventing a believable story to gain trust and access.

- **Mirroring & Rapport Building:** Mimicking victim behavior to build trust and manipulate.

- **Reverse Social Engineering:** Sabotage → advertise help → victim seeks assistance → attacker collects info.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**🏢 3. Physical Social Engineering Attacks**

Involve **in-person deception** or physical actions to gain unauthorized access.

**Techniques Include:**

- **Tailgating:** Following closely behind authorized personnel into a secure zone.

- **Piggybacking:** Convincing someone to hold the door or bypass authentication.

- **Unannounced Visitors:** Pretending to be contractors (e.g., electricians, delivery personnel).

- **Device Drops:** Leaving infected USBs or phones hoping staff plug them in.

- **Dumpster Diving:** Retrieving sensitive data from discarded documents or devices.

- **Shoulder Surfing:** Watching users enter PINs or passwords.

- **Eavesdropping:** Listening to confidential conversations or monitoring unguarded devices.

- **Lost Devices:** Deliberately leaving compromised media for discovery and use.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**🧨 4. Insider Threats & Malicious Actors**

Internal actors or plants who have legitimate access but act with malicious intent.

**Examples:**

- **Malicious Insiders:** Employees stealing data or sabotaging systems.

- **Planted Rogue Employees:** Hired with intent to spy or steal.

- **Help Desk Exploitation:** Targeting overly helpful support staff to extract credentials or access.

### Countermeasures

To protect against these attacks:

- Train employees on impersonation, vishing, and tailgating tactics.

- Enforce visitor escort policies and ID badge verification.

- Apply MFA and least privilege access control.

- Monitor physical and digital behaviors for anomalies.

- Implement strong verification protocols and phishing simulations.

### Mitigation Checklist

These controls focus on detecting, preventing, and responding to **human-centric manipulation**, such as impersonation, tailgating, vishing, and insider threats.

**1. Identity & Access Verification**

- Enforce **strict identity checks** for all personnel and visitors (e.g., government ID, employee badge).

- Use **multi-factor authentication (MFA)** for both physical and system access.

- Regularly **review and restrict access rights** using the principle of least privilege.

**2. Security Awareness & Training**

- Conduct **mandatory training** for employees and help desk staff to detect and respond to social engineering (phishing, impersonation, reverse engineering).

- Promote a **“challenge and verify”** culture where staff question unfamiliar individuals and requests.

- Run **simulated attacks** (e.g., phishing/vishing drills) to test awareness and reinforce training.

**3. Physical Security Controls**

- Deploy layered access mechanisms:\
  ‣ Turnstiles, **mantraps**, and security guards at entry points.\
  ‣ Enforce **visitor sign-in, escort, and badge** policies.

- Install **privacy screens** and design workspaces to minimize risks like shoulder surfing.

- Monitor **unauthorized device use** (e.g., block or restrict USB ports).

**4. Secure Disposal of Sensitive Material**

- Shred paper documents with PII or business-sensitive data.

- Use **locked disposal bins** for confidential waste.

- Digitally wipe or destroy **storage media** (e.g., USBs, hard drives) before disposal.

**5. Monitoring, Detection & Response**

- Enable and **review CCTV** at all physical access points.

- **Log and audit access records** (badge entries, system logins).

- Implement **user behavior analytics (UBA)** to detect anomalies from insiders.

- Establish **clear incident reporting protocols** for suspected social engineering attempts.

**6. Secure Communication Practices**

- Use **encryption and secure channels** (e.g., VPNs, TLS-protected email) for sensitive discussions.

- Discourage use of personal or unverified communication platforms.

Phishing

Phishing involves sending fraudulent emails that appear to be from legitimate sources, urging recipients to perform actions like clicking links or opening attachments. These links may lead to malicious websites designed to steal sensitive information or infect devices with malware.

**Key Points:**

- Attackers impersonate trusted websites (e.g., Yahoo, Facebook) to trick users into submitting passwords or account IDs.

- Phishing emails may contain malware-infected attachments.

**Prevention Tips:**

- Use spam filters and email security solutions.

- Only share personal information on secure (HTTPS) websites.

- Avoid downloading files or attachments from unknown senders.

- Never send financial information via email.

- Be cautious of email links asking for personal info.

- Ignore pop-up windows requesting personal data.

**Spear Phishing**

- A targeted form of phishing aimed at specific individuals, often with high privileges like system admins or executives, using personalized social engineering.

**Homograph Attacks**

- Attackers create fake websites with URLs visually similar to legitimate sites by substituting characters (e.g., using "а" Cyrillic letter instead of "a"). Users are deceived into entering credentials on these spoofed sites.

**Spamming**

- Sending unsolicited bulk messages, often used to distribute phishing links or malware.

You get an email that looks like it is from someone you know.

It seems to be from one of your company’s vendors and asks that you click on a link to update your business account. Should you click? Maybe it looks like it is from your boss and asks for your network password. Should you reply? In either case, probably not. These may be phishing attempts.

How phishing works?

- **You get an email or text** It seems to be from someone you know, and it asks you to click a link, or give your password, business bank account, or other sensitive information.

- **It looks real** It is easy to spoof logos and make up fake email addresses. Scammers use familiar company names or pretend to be someone you know.

- **It is urgent** the message pressures you to act now — or something bad will happen.

- **What happens next** If you click on a link, scammers can install ransomware or other programs that can lock you out of your data and spread to the entire company network. If you share passwords, scammers now have access to all those accounts.

What you can do?

Before you click on a link or share any of your sensitive business information:

- **Check it out** Look up the website or phone number for the company or person behind the text or email. Make sure that you are getting the real company and not about to download malware or talk to a scammer.

- **Talk to someone** Talking to a colleague might help you figure out if the request is real or a phishing attempt.

- **Make a call if you are not sure** Pick up the phone and call that vendor, colleague, or client who sent the email. Confirm that they really need information from you. Use a number you know to be correct, not the number in the email or text

How to your business protect?

- **Back up your data** Regularly back up your data and make sure those backups are not connected to the network. That way, if a phishing attack happens and hackers get to your network, you can restore your data. Make data backup part of your routine business operations.

- **Keep your security up to date** Always install the latest patches and updates. Look for additional means of protection, like email authentication and intrusion prevention software, and set them to update automatically on your computers. On mobile devices, you may have to do it manually.

- **Alert your staff** Share with them this information. Keep in mind that phishing scammers change their tactics often, so make sure you include tips for spotting the latest phishing schemes in your regular training.

- **Deploy a safety net** Use email authentication technology to help prevent phishing emails from reaching your company’s inboxes in the first place.

What if you fall for a phishing scheme?

- **Alert others** Talk to your colleagues and share your experience. Phishing attacks often happen to more than one person in a company

- **Limit the damage** Immediately change any compromised passwords and disconnect from the network any computer or device that is infected with malware.

### Malicious apps

You're absolutely right — instead of limiting by platform (mobile vs. desktop), the modern approach is to **categorize all such threats under a unified category based on behavior and intent**, not just the device type.

**✅ New Modern Category: "Malicious Application-Based Attacks"**

This category covers **any software intentionally designed or repurposed to deceive, exploit, or compromise users or systems**, regardless of whether it's targeting mobile phones, desktops, laptops, or servers.

**🧠 Definition:**

**Malicious Application-Based Attacks** are software-based threats that exploit user trust through deceptive apps, repackaged software, or fake utilities to gain unauthorized access, harvest data, or compromise system integrity across any platform (mobile, desktop, or server).

**🔍 Subtypes and Examples:**

| **Subcategory** | **Description** | **Typical Targets** |
|----|----|----|
| **Malicious Apps Published to App Stores or Web** | Attackers develop fake or copycat apps mimicking real ones (e.g., banking, utilities, games) to trick users into installing malware. | Android, iOS, Windows, Linux |
| **Repackaged Legitimate Software** | Original software is modified to include malicious payloads (backdoors, spyware) and redistributed. | All platforms |
| **Fake Security/Utility Applications** | Pretend to be antivirus, disk cleaners, VPNs, etc., while harvesting data or disabling security features. | Desktop/mobile systems |
| **Trojanized Installers** | Software bundled with malicious code under the guise of legitimate tools (e.g., cracked software, free tools). | Primarily desktops, servers |
| **Credential Stealing Apps** | Applications that capture login credentials, OTPs, or financial data through fake login screens or SMS/email interception. | Banking apps, 2FA interceptors |
| **Sideloaded Apps via Social Engineering** | Users are tricked into installing an app manually from outside trusted app stores, often using phishing or fake support. | Android, Windows (via EXEs) |
| **App-Based Persistence or Surveillance Tools** | Apps that install persistent malware or spyware (e.g., stalkerware, RATs). | BYOD, corporate endpoints |

**Defense Strategies Across Platforms:**

- **Application Whitelisting** and EDR on endpoints

- **Restrict sideloading** and enforce **store validation**

- Use **sandboxing/virtualization** for untrusted apps

- Enforce **least privilege** and **runtime permission checks**

- **Behavioural analysis tools** for apps (static + dynamic analysis)

- Deploy **Mobile Device Management (MDM)** or **Endpoint Detection & Response (EDR)** solutions

- User awareness training: avoid suspicious installers and verify app sources

**Why This Category Matters:**

- Avoids splitting detection and response strategies by platform

- Reflects the reality of **cross-platform attack techniques**

- Matches attacker behaviour: **malicious apps are no longer platform-bound**

# Countermeasures

Social engineering countermeasures are **proactive controls**, both technical and behavioural, aimed at **preventing, detecting, and responding** to attacks that exploit human behaviour — such as phishing, vishing, impersonation, and physical intrusion.

**1. User Awareness & Training**

- Conduct regular security awareness training on phishing, vishing, pretexting, baiting, and impersonation.

- Simulate phishing emails to test and reinforce behaviour.

- Require employees to **acknowledge security policies** during onboarding and refresher sessions.

- Teach staff to **verify requests** through a **secondary communication channel**.

**2. Security Policies & Procedures**

- Enforce **password security policies**:

  - Minimum length, character complexity, periodic rotation.

  - Lockouts on multiple failed login attempts.

- Define and enforce **data classification levels** (e.g., Confidential, Internal, Public).

- Require background checks and structured offboarding.

- Apply **incident response plans** for suspected social engineering attempts.

**3. Technical Controls**

- Enforce **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** across all key systems.

- Use **email filtering, anti-virus, anti-phishing**, and endpoint protection solutions.

- Implement **access controls** using the **principle of least privilege**.

- Apply **logging and behavioural monitoring** to detect anomalies (e.g., odd login patterns).

**4. Physical & Operational Security**

- Issue **ID badges**, restrict visitor access, and monitor for tailgating.

- Escort visitors in sensitive zones; enforce **shred/destroy policies** for sensitive paperwork.

- Regularly audit access to physical devices and server rooms.

**5. User Best Practices**

- **Do not click** suspicious links or open unexpected attachments.

- Never **share passwords or sensitive data** through unverified emails or calls.

- Be wary of emails with:

  - Generic greetings (“Dear User”), urgent language, spelling mistakes, spoofed domains.

- Promptly **report any suspicious activity** to IT/security teams.

**6. Detection & Anti-Phishing Tools**

- **Netcraft Toolbar** – community-driven anti-phishing browser extension.

- **PhishTank** – open phishing database for integration with filters and email protection systems.

**7. Identity Theft Prevention**

- Shred documents containing personal data.

- Monitor financial and credit reports.

- Do not overshare personal info online or on calls.

- Protect mail and secure personal identifiers.

🔐 Preventing Insider Threats

**Key Controls:**

- **Separation and Rotation of Duties**: Limits long-term control over critical systems by a single individual.

- **Principle of Least Privilege**: Users get only the minimum access required for their roles.

- **Controlled Access**: Use of access control lists, role-based permissions, and just-in-time access.

- **Logging and Auditing**: Monitor and record user actions for anomaly detection and accountability.

- **Legal Agreements**: Enforce confidentiality and IP protection via contracts and legal consequences.

- **Data Archiving**: Secure backups and version control of critical files to ensure recovery and integrity.

**How would you conduct security testing on physical devices like ATMs or smart parking meters?**

- **Reconnaissance:** Identify manufacturer, model, OS, and communication protocols (e.g., cellular, Wi-Fi).

- **Physical inspection:** Check for easy access panels, USB ports, debug ports, or maintenance interfaces.

- **Firmware analysis:** Attempt to extract and analyze firmware for vulnerabilities.

- **Network testing:** Intercept and analyze communication between device and backend systems.

- **Hardware hacking:** Use lockpicking or device disassembly to gain physical access to internals.

**What role does lockpicking play in physical security testing?**

- Lockpicking is a **non-destructive technique** to assess the effectiveness of physical locks and barriers.

- It helps determine how easily an attacker could bypass physical access controls without leaving evidence.

- Enables testers to evaluate risks related to unauthorized entry into sensitive areas or devices.

- Supports recommendations for upgrading locks or adding additional security layers (e.g., alarms, CCTV).

**After compromising a smart device (e.g., a smart thermometer in a casino), how would you pivot to access a high-value target such as a high-roller database, and then exit stealthily?**

- **Pivoting steps:**

  - Perform **internal network reconnaissance** from the compromised device (e.g., scanning subnet, identifying other hosts).

  - Enumerate connected systems and look for weak services or open management ports.

  - Exploit vulnerabilities or weak credentials on lateral targets like servers or admin workstations.

  - Clean logs or use legitimate admin tools to cover tracks.

  - Use encrypted tunnels or anonymizing proxies to avoid detection during data exfiltration.

  - Remove or disable any implanted malware or backdoors after data collection.

  - Exit the network quietly, maintaining persistence only if required by mission scope.
